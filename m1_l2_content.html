1. Introduction to the STM32 Ecosystem

STMicroelectronics (ST) released the STM32 family in 2007. Since then, it has become one of the most popular 32-bit microcontroller families globally, dominating automotive, IoT, and industrial applications. As a Rust embedded engineer, you will inevitably encounter STM32s.

However, "STM32" is not a single chip. It is a massive family of over 1,000 different microcontrollers, all based on the ARM Cortex-M processor core. In this lesson, we will demystify the naming conventions, architecture, and explain which boards are best supported by the Rust ecosystem.

2. Understanding ARM Cortex-M Cores

STMicroelectronics does not design the CPU core. They license the Cortex-M processor design from ARM Holdings and then wrap it with their own proprietary memory, flash controllers, and hardware peripherals (like Timers, ADCs, and UARTs).

The Cortex-M family is optimized for deterministic, real-time embedded systems. In the Rust ecosystem, we target these cores using different compile targets:

  • Cortex-M0 / M0+ (Target: thumbv6m-none-eabi): Entry-level, ultra-low power. Found in the STM32F0 and G0 lines. Extremely limited instruction set.
  • Cortex-M3 (Target: thumbv7m-none-eabi): The original balance of performance and power. Found in the legendary STM32F103 (the "Blue Pill").
  • Cortex-M4 (Target: thumbv7em-none-eabi(hf)): Adds DSP (Digital Signal Processing) instructions and an optional FPU (Floating Point Unit). The standard for modern industrial IoT. Found in the STM32F4 series.
  • Cortex-M7 (Target: thumbv7em-none-eabihf): High-performance core with superscalar pipelines and advanced caching. Found in the STM32F7 and H7 series.

3. The STM32 Product Lines

ST organizes their chips into distinct families based on their primary use case. When you look at an STM32 part number (e.g., STM32F401RET6), the first letter after "STM32" tells you its family.

The F-Series (Foundation & High Performance)

This is the bread and butter of the STM32 world. The STM32F4 is arguably the most supported chip in the entire embedded Rust ecosystem. If you are buying a board to learn Rust, buy an STM32F401RE or STM32F411RE Nucleo board.

The L-Series (Ultra-Low Power)

Designed for battery-operated IoT devices. They feature aggressive sleep modes, running for years on a coin cell battery. In Rust, you will rely heavily on the WFI (Wait For Interrupt) assembly instruction to drop these chips into deep sleep.

The G-Series and H-Series

The G-Series focuses on advanced analog peripherals (high-res ADCs, math accelerators). The H-Series (H7) are absolute monsters, running at 480MHz with Megabytes of RAM, often used for graphical interfaces and heavy AI/ML on the edge.

4. Decoding the Part Number

As a firmware engineer, you must know exactly what chip you are targeting so you can configure Cargo and your memory.x linker script correctly. Let's decode STM32F401RET6:

  • STM32: 32-bit ARM Cortex microcontroller.
  • F: Mainstream Foundation series.
  • 401: Cortex-M4 core, Entry-level line.
  • R: Pin count (R = 64 pins, V = 100 pins, Z = 144 pins).
  • E: Flash size (C = 256KB, E = 512KB, G = 1MB).
  • T: Package type (T = LQFP).
  • 6: Temperature range (-40°C to 85°C).

For this chip, you would set your Rust linker script to have exactly 512KB of Flash.

5. Rust Support Across the Families

Rust support for STM32 is driven by the stm32-rs community. They maintain the PACs (Peripheral Access Crates) for almost every STM32 chip ever made.

However, HAL (Hardware Abstraction Layer) support varies. The HALs for F1, F3, F4, and G0 are extremely mature and production-ready. The HALs for newer lines like H7 or WBA are under active development. When designing a new product around Rust, always check the HAL completion matrix on GitHub before finalizing hardware selection.

6. Summary

The STM32 family provides an incredible range of microcontrollers. By understanding the ARM Cortex-M architecture and how ST labels their chips, you can confidently select the right target triple in Rust, configure your linker scripts, and choose a board with mature open-source driver support.
